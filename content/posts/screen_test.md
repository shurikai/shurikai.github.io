When is a screen a test? Every time, if your interviewer is good at their
job.

I was reminded of this recently, when I had a first interview with a
company I hadn't spoken with before. Going into this first round, I did
the exact thing you should never do when facing the unknown. I made
assumptions about the interview based on prior interviews that I had been
through. The first screen is never terribly technical, right?

Halfway through this interview, I was sweating about how the interviewer
was going deeper than surface-level, finding myself rambling and trying to
put together official-sounding textbook answers to his questions. Was it
bad that he asked this type of question? Of course not. But it was bad that
I went in not expecting it. That was totally on me.

I kept my composure and leaned on my years of experience to pull me through,
and I impressed the recruiter enough to be moved on to the second round.
After the first interview, I realized that I needed to make a significant
change to my preparation for the next interview.

This time, I put together a set of questions that seemed to be possible,
or even likely, then practiced answering those questions. I assumed
nothing this time, and looked deeper into my technical background. I
found myself studying my own resume like I was getting ready to take an
AP exam on The History of Jason. While I was brushing up on what makes
me unique, I decided to apply that approach to the next round's interviewer.
I found him on LinkedIn and studied up on his work background and job
history.

It turns out that each interview serves not only as practice, but also as
recon for the future rounds. Go in ready to discuss anything in your 
resume, and pay close attention to what the interviewer is telling you.
This company was looking for someone who had solid fundamentals as a 
backend developer, and who could speak from authority and with confidence.
I brought that confidence into my second interview, where I felt great
and answered everything that came my way. 

Every interview tells you what you need to know about the next one. Pay
attention and learn.
